# ECCCH Components

From an assessment perspective, whether the ECCCH is evaluated as an integrated whole or through its individual parts, the digital ecosystem can be understood as a set of interrelated components that together enable heritage professionals and researchers to access, manage, reuse, and enrich cultural heritage data, tools, and services within a secure and federated environment. 

These components define the scope of what can be assessed and are therefore essential for framing assessment activities. An indicator may apply to a specific and delimited part of the cloud, such as a particular vertical application; to transversal elements spanning multiple resources, such as datasets accessible through the ECCCH or ontologies integrated into the ecosystem; or to the cloud as a whole, for example when assessing overall impact or sustainability. 

In this document, these scopes or objects of assessment are referred to as components. The concept is closely related to the notion of resources introduced in Deliverable D3.1, and the two terms are used here interchangeably. In this context, however, components or resources refer primarily to the object of assessment from the perspective of scope, regardless of whether a given resource is a candidate for integration into the cloud. 

It should also be noted that other deliverables use the term Cloud Components in a narrower sense, referring to the core architectural elements and foundational services of the shared infrastructure with which digital resources must interact or integrate. These include, for example, the Authentication and Authorization Infrastructure, the Knowledge Base, the Service Entry Point, and supporting services for data storage, processing, and monitoring. 
There are multiple valid ways to classify the different components of the ECCCH. The following subsections present several classifications. These classifications are not intended to be exhaustive, and reflect the conception of the ECCCH at the time this deliverable was written. Alternative classifications may exist, and the elements considered part of the ECCCH are expected to evolve as the ECCCH becomes operational and matures. Nevertheless, the classifications presented below provide an overall view of the ECCCH based on its components, and therefore of the elements that may be subject to evaluation.

- [Main ECCCH components](main_components.md) — The first and most basic classification uses the nature of the components, such as data, software applications and services. This criterion is adopted because the nature of each component strongly influences how it can be assessed.
- [Components by evaluable units](components_by_evaluable_units.md) — One way to refine the classification above is to introduce additional levels that identify concrete, individually assessable resources within each component type. This classification is not intended to be exhaustive, and compiling a comprehensive catalog of such resources is beyond the scope of this document. Instead, we provide illustrative examples at the lowest level of the hierarchy, including specific heritage digital twins, ontologies, vertical applications, courses, and workflows. These individual resources represent meaningful and well-defined units that can be independently selected as subjects of evaluation activities. For instance, when planning a usability study, it is appropriate to delimit the scope to one such specific resource.
